About me

I consider myself a generalist, passionate about using teaching, research, and engineering to help folks. I love teaching, and preparing assignments and presentations that enhance learning experiences. I’m very fortunate to teach analytical and planning methods and GIS for landscape architecture and urban planning students. I strive to make each semester a great and special one; my goal is to equip students with fundamental research methods while fostering their analytical thinking and programming skills. I know that it might feel challenging at times, but these are important tools for researchers and planners. Whether it’s analyzing data, modeling scenarios, or creating visualizations, statistics and spatial analysis empower informed and impactful decisions. I encourage students to keep experimenting, exploring, and asking questions; the more they practice, the more confident they become. After all, every expert was once a beginner. I’m a coding and reproducible research enthusiast, committed to employ and promote the use of open-source software and publicly available data to enhance collaboration and make education and research more accessible and transparent.

Short bio

Alexander Abuabara received his B.Sc. in Civil Engineering from the University of São Paulo (Brazil, 2001), his M.Sc. in Production Engineering from the Federal University of São Carlos (Brazil, 2006), and his Ph.D. in Urban and Regional Science from Texas A&M University (USA, 2022). He has worked on forecasting models and logistics for one of the world’s largest citrus juice companies (2002–2005) and contributed to the TAMU/HRRC/TTI Hurricane Evacuation Studies team (2015–2025), supporting the modeling of hazard exposure, analysis of household evacuation behavior, and delineation of evacuation zones for planning and risk mitigation. Before joining the faculty in 2022, he worked as a NIST-funded postdoctoral research associate at Texas A&M University modeling federal individual assistance. During the 2024–2025 academic year, he serves as Faculty Senator for the College of Architecture. Dr. Abuabara teaches research and analytical methods and GIS, aiming to enhance students' analytical thinking and programming skills. He is currently developing a textbook on Bayesian statistics and probabilistic causal analysis using influence diagrams. His research interests include risk assessment modeling and decision-making analysis to better understand irrational beliefs, with applications in planning.

Teaching statement in brief

Teaching has always been one of the most energizing parts of my work, whether training colleagues as an engineer, mentoring students as a researcher, or leading my own classes. I genuinely want to be a great teacher, and that means always learning, adapting, and getting better. I’ve had semesters that went really well and others that challenged me. One time after getting my lowest student evaluations, I had to step back, reflect and rethink my approach. I realized that every group of students is different and what worked once might not work again. Since then I’ve evolve my approach, balancing structure with flexibility and authority with collaboration. I prioritize hands-on activities, applied learning that connects students to real-world problems and industries through applied projects, aiming to build both competence and confidence. My teaching philosophy is grounded in an authentic, sincere desire to be an outstanding educator who adapts, learns, and improves with every experience. Teaching for me extends beyond lectures; it includes mentoring, fostering peer learning, and creating opportunities for students to build meaningful portfolios and professional identities. Teaching isn’t just about delivering content; it’s about being present, responsive, and committed to the growth of each student.
