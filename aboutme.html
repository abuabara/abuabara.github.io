About me

I consider myself a generalist, passionate about using teaching, research, and engineering to help folks. I love teaching, and preparing assignments and presentations that enhance learning experiences. I’m very fortunate to teach analytical and planning methods and GIS for landscape architecture and urban planning students. I strive to make each semester a great and special one; my goal is to equip students with fundamental research methods while fostering their analytical thinking and programming skills. I know that it might feel challenging at times, but these are important tools for researchers and planners. Whether it’s analyzing data, modeling scenarios, or creating visualizations, statistics and spatial analysis empower informed and impactful decisions. I encourage students to keep experimenting, exploring, and asking questions; the more they practice, the more confident they become. After all, every expert was once a beginner. I’m a coding and reproducible research enthusiast, committed to employ and promote the use of open-source software and publicly available data to enhance collaboration and make education and research more accessible and transparent.

Short bio

Alexander Abuabara received his B.Sc. in Civil Engineering from the University of São Paulo (Brazil, 2001), his M.Sc. in Production Engineering from the Federal University of São Carlos (Brazil, 2006), and his Ph.D. in Urban and Regional Science from Texas A&M University (USA, 2022). He has worked on forecasting models and logistics for one of the world’s largest citrus juice companies (2002–2005) and contributed to the TAMU/HRRC/TTI Hurricane Evacuation Studies team (2015–2024), contributing in modeling hazard exposure, household evacuation behaviour, and mapping evacuation zones. Before joining the faculty in 2022, he worked as a NIST-funded postdoctoral research associate at Texas A&M University, modeling federal individual and public assistances. Dr. Abuabara teaches research and analytical methods and GIS, aiming to enhance students' analytical thinking and programming skills. He is currently developing a textbook on Bayesian statistics and probabilistic causal analysis using influence diagrams. During the 2024–2025 academic year, he serves as Faculty Senator for the College of Architecture. His research interests include risk assessment modeling and decision-making analysis to better understand irrational beliefs, with applications in planning.
